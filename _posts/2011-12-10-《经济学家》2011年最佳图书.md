   <p>
    <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/069115631X/ref=as_li_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=390957&amp;creativeASIN=069115631X&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;tag=oceintelleweb-20&amp;linkId=5XKTYNQVVWRTH64Y">

    </a>
    <br/>
    <a href="http://www.economist.com/node/18741382">
     <em>
      Exceptional People: How Migration Shaped Our World and Will Define Our Future
     </em>
    </a>
    . By Ian Goldin, Geoffrey Cameron and Meera Balarajan. Princeton University Press; 352 pages; $35 and ￡24.95
    <br/>
    Few things will affect our future more than migration. By calculating the how, where and why of future labour shortages, the authors analyse the costs and benefits of human migration.
    <br/>
    <span id="more-1196">
    </span>
    <br/>
    <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/1595588809/ref=as_li_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=390957&amp;creativeASIN=1595588809&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;tag=oceintelleweb-20&amp;linkId=TEGRD23G664CA36U">
     <img alt="Tide Players: The Movers and Shakers of a Rising China" src="http://ecx.images-amazon.com/images/I/41fm0dI6YFL.jpg"/>
    </a>
    <br/>
    <a href="http://www.amazon.cn/gp/product/1595588809/ref=as_li_ss_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;camp=536&amp;creative=3132&amp;creativeASIN=1595588809&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;tag=ideobook">
     <em>
      Tide Players: The Movers and Shakers of a Rising China
     </em>
    </a>
    . By Jianying Zha. The New Press; 240 pages; $24.95 and ￡18.99
    <br/>
    A highly readable study from a Beijing-born writer for the New Yorker about China’s “tide players”, the intellectual and entrepreneurial pragmatists who prosper by pushing at the boundaries of what the state permits while taking care never to overstep the mark.
   </p>
   <p>
    <a href="http://www.amazon.cn/gp/product/1610391071/ref=as_li_ss_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;camp=536&amp;creative=3132&amp;creativeASIN=1610391071&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;tag=ideobook">
     <img alt="Dancing in the Glory of Monsters: The Collapse of the Congo and the Great War of Africa" src="http://ecx.images-amazon.com/images/I/615x9UCwmDL.jpg"/>
    </a>
    <br/>
    <a href="http://www.economist.com/node/18617876">
     <em>
      Dancing in the Glory of Monsters: The Collapse of the Congo and the Great War of Africa
     </em>
    </a>
    . By Jason Stearns. PublicAffairs; 400 pages; $28.99 and ￡18.99
    <br/>
    A serious account of the social and political forces behind one of the most violent clashes of modern times—a 15-year war in Congo that has spilled over into neighbouring countries and claimed as many as 5m lives—by one of its most meticulous and empathetic observers.
   </p>
   <p>
    <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/000743202X/ref=as_li_ss_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;tag=oceintelleweb-20&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=390957&amp;creativeASIN=000743202X">
     <img alt="Cables from Kabul: The Inside Story of the West’s Afghanistan Campaign" src="http://ecx.images-amazon.com/images/I/51B5DU4KAXL.jpg"/>
    </a>
    <br/>
    <a href="http://www.economist.com/node/21528225">
     <em>
      Cables from Kabul: The Inside Story of the West’s Afghanistan Campaign
     </em>
    </a>
    . By Sherard Cowper-Coles. Harper Press; 352 pages; ￡25
    <br/>
    A former British ambassador to Afghanistan—and an outspoken early critic of Western policy—breaks out of the static group-think and argues that the current military-led strategy in the country is fatally flawed.
   </p>
   <p>
    <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/0141044594/ref=as_li_ss_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;tag=oceintelleweb-20&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=390957&amp;creativeASIN=0141044594">
     <img alt="The 9/11 Wars" src="http://ecx.images-amazon.com/images/I/41BvvoGbVfL.jpg"/>
    </a>
    <br/>
    <a href="http://www.economist.com/node/21528225">
     <em>
      The 9/11 Wars
     </em>
    </a>
    . By Jason Burke. Penguin Global; 709 pages; $20. Allen Lane; ￡30
    <br/>
    An ambitious attempt to knit into a coherent whole the sprawling fabric of the “war on terror”. Jason Burke of the Guardian, who has covered Afghanistan, Pakistan and the Middle East through every phase of the conflict, focuses on the ordinary people affected by the troubles rather than on decision-makers in far-off capitals.
   </p>
   <p>
    <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/1610390210/ref=as_li_ss_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;tag=oceintelleweb-20&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=390957&amp;creativeASIN=1610390210">
     <img alt="Pakistan: A Hard Country" src="http://ecx.images-amazon.com/images/I/51pSAX7TVIL.jpg"/>
    </a>
    <br/>
    <a href="http://www.economist.com/node/18526715?story_id=18526715">
     <em>
      Pakistan: A Hard Country
     </em>
    </a>
    . By Anatol Lieven. PublicAffairs; 558 pages; $35. Allen Lane; ￡30
    <br/>
    A former Times reporter who now teaches at King’s College London, Anatol Lieven has travelled widely through Pakistan talking to generals, shopkeepers, farmers, lawyers and bureaucrats. A book that captures all the drama and colour of this complex Muslim nation.
   </p>
   <p>
    <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/0470825863/ref=as_li_ss_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;tag=oceintelleweb-20&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=390957&amp;creativeASIN=0470825863">
     <img alt="Red Capitalism: The Fragile Financial Foundation of China’s Extraordinary Rise" src="http://ecx.images-amazon.com/images/I/51xgIkOAXdL.jpg"/>
    </a>
    <br/>
    <a href="http://www.economist.com/node/17672932">
     <em>
      Red Capitalism: The Fragile Financial Foundation of China’s Extraordinary Rise
     </em>
    </a>
    . By Carl Walter and Fraser Howie. Wiley; 250 pages; $29.95 and ￡19.99
    <br/>
    Two bankers with years of experience in China shine an unprecedented light on the remarkable 32-year effort to build the country’s financial system—on its vices, virtues and many conflicts of interest.
   </p>

   <p>

    <br/>

   <p>
    <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/0307267679/ref=as_li_ss_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;tag=oceintelleweb-20&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=390957&amp;creativeASIN=0307267679">

    </a>
    <br/>
    <a href="http://www.economist.com/node/21536546">
     <em>
      Blue Nights
     </em>
    </a>
    . By Joan Didion. Knopf; 208 pages; $25. Fourth Estate; ￡14.99
    <br/>
    Even when Joan Didion writes about the hard drama of her own life, particularly the sudden death of her husband followed by the death of her only daughter, her memoirs manage to be larger than her own grief. This is a beautiful book, tragic and profound.
   </p>
   <p>
    <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/0375507485/ref=as_li_ss_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;tag=oceintelleweb-20&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=390957&amp;creativeASIN=0375507485">

    <a href="http://www.economist.com/node/21536550">
     <em>
      Van Gogh: The Life
     </em>
    </a>
    . By Steven Naifeh and Gregory White Smith. Random House; 953 pages; $40. Profile; ￡30
    <br/>
    An ambitious, original book, by two energetic art-history researchers, which describes the sublime Impressionist as a lonely, syphilitic boozer who bit the hands that fed him.
   </p>
   <p>
    <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/1594203091/ref=as_li_ss_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;tag=oceintelleweb-20&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=390957&amp;creativeASIN=1594203091">

      Charles Dickens: A Life
     </em>
    </a>

    <br/>
    This is a superb life of Britain’s greatest novelist by its greatest literary biographer.
   </p>
   <p>
    <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/0307265587/ref=as_li_ss_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;tag=oceintelleweb-20&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=390957&amp;creativeASIN=0307265587">
     <img alt="Ghosts by Daylight: A Memoir of Love, War and Redemption" src="http://ecx.images-amazon.com/images/I/51odVUtxtdL.jpg"/>
    </a>
    <br/>
    <a href="http://www.economist.com/node/21524804">
     <em>
      Ghosts by Daylight: A Memoir of Love, War and Redemption
     </em>
    </a>
    . By Janine di Giovanni. Knopf; 304 pages; $26.95. Bloomsbury; ￡16.99
    <br/>
    A beautifully written memoir, by a Paris-based American war reporter, about the pain of adjusting to normal life after being exposed to the intensity of battle.
   </p>
   <p>
    <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/1608195236/ref=as_li_ss_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;tag=oceintelleweb-20&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=390957&amp;creativeASIN=1608195236">

     <em>
      The Paper Garden: An Artist Begins her Life’s Work at 72
     </em>
    </a>

    <br/>
    How Mary Delaney—aristocrat, gardener, woman of fashion and friend to Jonathan Swift and King George III—created the “Flora Delanica”. Less a biography, more an extended prose poem.

    <br/>

   <p>
    <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/0525952713/ref=as_li_ss_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;tag=oceintelleweb-20&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=390957&amp;creativeASIN=0525952713">

    <a href="http://www.economist.com/node/18276872">
     <em>

     </em>
    </a>
    . By Tyler Cowen. Dutton Adult; 128 pages; $12.95
    <br/>
    A small book full of big ideas about the historic changes wrought through education and innovation. An American economist offers plenty to think about for readers of every ideological stripe.
   </p>
   <p>
    <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/0805091203/ref=as_li_ss_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;tag=oceintelleweb-20&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=390957&amp;creativeASIN=0805091203">

     <em>

    <br/>

   </p>
   <p>
    <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/038552384X/ref=as_li_ss_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;tag=oceintelleweb-20&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=390957&amp;creativeASIN=038552384X">

     </em>
    </a>

    <br/>
    A rollercoaster account of how Goldman Sachs does business, and the best analysis yet of its increasingly tangled web of conflicts, by a master-storyteller.
   </p>
